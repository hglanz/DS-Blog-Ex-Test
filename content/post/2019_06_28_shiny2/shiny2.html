Shiny Recap

Yesterday we introduced R Shiny and how it allows you to build interactive web applications straight from R. We saw a few examples highlighting the wondrous interactivtiy of exploratory data analysis, data visualization, and data analysis that it enables. If you didn’t catch yesterday’s post, check it out at https://teachdatascience.com/shiny1/ and be sure to go play around with some Shiny apps at RStudio’s gallery at https://shiny.rstudio.com/gallery/.

Shiny is too powerful not to be used in as many ways as possible. Today’s post will detail a few of the major ways it can be integrated into the classroom.

Shiny Apps for Student Users

With Shiny, instructors with knowledge of R now have the ability to create their own “applets” or demos to connect students with whatever dataset or data science topic they wish. The importance of tools like the Rossman/Chance applets and statkey cannot be overstated, but curriculum and activities no longer need to conform to whatever is out there. As educators and R users, most classroom activities that involve the computer in some way can be converted into an interactive Shiny app.

And these Shiny activities are not limited to introductory topics. To a very large extent, if it can be done in R then it can be incorporated into a Shiny app in some fashion. Though they’re using R on the backend, remember that these Shiny apps can be deployed to a website to be viewed and used within a browser…accessible anywhere there’s internet. Without access to a server at your institution/organization, shinyapps.io by RStudio will host your Shiny app for free. If the resources accompanying the free usage tier are not sufficient, remember that many of RStudio’s pro products are free for educators.

Especially cool is the unprecedented ability to connect students novel methods and research via Shiny. This article outlines a collaborative and interactive environment for students to learn about and apply new methods via Shiny apps.

Beyond statistical concepts like data visualization and analysis, Shiny apps are a great way to teach R skills themselves. The learnr package facilitates this process explicitly by making the construction of custom R tutorials easier than ever. Keep an eye out for a future blog post all about the learnr package!

Shiny Apps By Students

Give students a Shiny app and they’ll exhaust what it can do. Give students the ability and opportunity to build a Shiny app and the sky’s the limit!

Equipping students with statistical computing and programming skills empowers them to really own and drive their work with data. Add to that the creation of a Shiny app and there’s nothing they’ll feel they can’t do with data. We’ve had great success asking teams of students (~3) to create a Shiny app as a final project. Not only does this give students fantastic practice with R, but the result is a product that they can literally show off to peers and prospective advisors, employers, etc.

Student NASA Shiny App

This app was created by a team of three students as a final project and the goal was exploratory data analysis with a dataset of their choice. The app can be found here.

Based on the apps you saw yesterday on the gallery, you know that the default aesthetics are nothing special. These students not only found an interesting dataset and exciting ways to explore it, but had the enthusiasm to customize the theme of the page and add that gif on the last tab. It’s clear they had fun creating it!

This app was also created by a team of three students as a final project and they chose to build a tool for exploring different classification methods. The app can be found here

This one’s fun for its simultaneous simplicity and complexity. There are some somewhat advanced methods included which are nice to visualize, but the choice of datasets is the coup de grace by providing stunning clarity to how the different methods handle different data situations.

Beyond the Classroom

Whether it’s exposure to Shiny as a learning tool or the process of building one whole, students gain tremendous value from Shiny for what it could eventually provide their employer. Interactive visualizations and dashboards are all but ubiquitous in industry for both monitoring and gaining insight from a company’s data.
